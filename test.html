Raising the next ChatGPT

The UAE’s AI agenda aligns with the UAE Centennial 2071 Vision, aimed at sustainable development, digital transformation, and economic diversification. Credits: ADNOC

The UAE is expanding artificial intelligence education across all levels, making it a central part of its national development plan. Starting from the 2025–2026 school year, public schools will introduce AI lessons from kindergarten through Grade 12. The curriculum will cover AI principles, data handling, ethical questions, and their practical application in daily activities and future careers.

At the university level, over 44 AI-related degree programmes are provided. Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence (MBZUAI), a globally ranked research university in Abu Dhabi, plays a key role in advancing AI talent and innovations. Its projects include Jais (an Arabic-language AI model) and a large-scale system trained on 65B data points (used to improve how AI understands and generates text).

This education push is supported by rapid growth in digital infrastructure. The UAE is investing $1.9B into data centres (by 2028), aiming to support AI systems, cloud services, and secure digital operations nationwide.

 

Protecting nature with tech

The emirate has received international recognition for its commitment, including UNESCO Biosphere Reserve designations. Credits: Aletihad

Abu Dhabi is emerging as a global model for conservation at a time when biodiversity is under threat worldwide. Through the Zayed Network of Nature Reserves, the emirate is protecting endangered species and ecosystems critical to climate resilience and long-term sustainability.

Launched in 2020, the network includes over 19 protected areas and spans more than 30% of the emirate’s land. These areas shelter endangered species such as the Arabian oryx, sand gazelle, dugong (a marine mammal similar to a manatee), and green sea turtle.

Beyond wildlife, the network protects coastal ecosystems like mangroves and wetlands that serve as “blue carbon sinks” (natural environments that absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere), helping to fight climate change. These areas also support food security by sustaining local fisheries.

The reserves are managed using advanced technology. The Environment Agency - Abu Dhabi uses satellite imaging, drones, wireless animal trackers, and computer vision systems (which can automatically interpret video footage) to monitor wildlife and habitat changes.

Place to visit

The experience also helps to fund Ski Dubai's penguin conservation, education, and research partners. Credits: My Dubai Pass

Ski Dubai → Ever seen snowfall in the desert? Ski Dubai, inside Mall of the Emirates, provides a complete winter experience with indoor ski slopes, real snow, and sub-zero temperatures year-round. The attraction features everything from skiing and snowboarding lessons to snow hiking, zorbing, bobsled rides, and even a frozen zipline.

Don’t miss: Meet the resident penguins (both king and gentoo species) as part of Ski Dubai’s conservation programme.

Where to eat

Issei → Located in the Radisson Hotel at Damac Hills, Issei serves Nikkei cuisine, a fusion of Japanese and Peruvian flavors. Expect bold dishes made with ingredients like aji amarillo, Andean potatoes, and corn, paired with miso, ginger, and soy. The menu blends precision with heat for a truly distinct culinary experience.
